# Insurance Glossary — Plain-Language Terms

## Premium
The amount charged for the insurance, shown as an annual figure (and a monthly equivalent). It's calculated from a base rate adjusted by risk factors from your answers.

## Deductible
The amount the insured pays out of pocket per claim before the insurer pays the rest. A higher deductible lowers the premium but means more cost per claim.

## Sum insured / coverage amount
The total value being insured (e.g. buildings + contents + equipment). For many products the premium is driven by the sum insured.

## Aggregate limit
The most the insurer will pay across all claims in the policy year (used on liability and cyber products).

## Liability limit
The maximum the policy will pay for a single liability claim or in total (e.g. $1M / $2M / $5M).

## Accept / Decline / Refer
The three quote outcomes. **Accept** = within appetite, premium shown. **Decline** = outside appetite, with reasons. **Refer** = needs an underwriter's review before a decision.

## Bind
To convert an accepted quote into an active policy. In InsureFlow the broker presses "Buy This Policy" to bind.

## Policy term
The length of cover — all InsureFlow policies are 12 months, with effective and expiry dates recorded on binding.

## Underwriting
The process of assessing a risk to decide whether to insure it and on what terms. Automatic rules handle most quotes; borderline ones are referred to a human underwriter.

## Endorsement
A change or addition to the standard policy wording (e.g. an extra coverage or an exclusion). Many are documented in the knowledge base.

## Exclusion
Something the policy does not cover (e.g. war, nuclear, certain pollution). See "Coverage & Common Exclusions."

## RC vs ACV
**Replacement Cost (RC)** pays to replace an item with a new equivalent. **Actual Cash Value (ACV)** pays replacement cost minus depreciation. RC costs more but pays more at claim time.

## Guaranteed Replacement Cost
A dwelling coverage option that rebuilds the home even if the cost exceeds the stated amount, subject to conditions.

## Sum at risk / exposure
The potential loss the insurer is exposed to — for example, the total premium of referred quotes is the underwriter's "premium at risk."

## Loss history / prior claims
Past claims or losses, usually over the last 5 years. A heavier loss history raises the premium and can trigger a referral.

## Short-rate cancellation
A mid-term cancellation where the refund is reduced by a penalty (less than a simple pro-rata refund). See "Cancellations & Refunds."

## Named perils vs comprehensive
**Named perils** covers only the specific events listed in the policy. **Comprehensive** (all-risk) covers everything except what's excluded — broader, and usually pricier.

## Mortgagee / loss payee
A lender or other party with a financial interest in the insured property, named on the policy to be paid in the event of a loss.
